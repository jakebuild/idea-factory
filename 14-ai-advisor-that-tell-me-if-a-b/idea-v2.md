# Idea #14: AI Book Advisor — Personalized Verdict in 30 Seconds (Improved)

## Version: v2
**Date:** 2026-03-25 21:30
**Status:** improved

## Original Idea
AI advisor that tell me if a book is worth reading based on my taste, snap cover or search by title, get personalized verdict in 30 seconds

## What Changed and Why

- **Cut camera/OCR entirely from v1.** The critique was blunt: camera recognition will eat 2 weeks of solo dev time, cost money per scan via Vision APIs, and introduce a dozen UX failure modes (bad lighting, worn covers, foreign editions). Title search + Google Books API delivers 90% of the value with 10% of the complexity. Camera becomes a v2 feature after the core loop is validated.

- **Solved cold start with a mandatory Tinder-swipe onboarding quiz.** A new user swiping through 10–15 pre-curated books (loved / meh / haven't read) takes under 60 seconds and immediately seeds a taste profile. The critique identified this as the biggest churn risk — users who get a generic verdict on their first try uninstall. The swipe quiz must happen before the first search, not be skippable, and must feel like a game not a form. Genre + author preferences are inferred automatically from swipes, not asked explicitly.

- **Replaced star ratings and summaries with a "well-read friend" verdict paragraph.** The critique nailed it: if the output is a star rating scraped from Goodreads with a thin AI wrapper, users feel cheated. The entire product is the verdict format — one opinionated short paragraph written in second person ("You'll love this if...", "Skip it if you..."), followed by a single bold call-to-action: Read It / Skip It / Maybe Later. No stars, no score, no Goodreads excerpt.

- **Went web-first (PWA), skipped App Store.** Native app distribution adds review cycles, TestFlight friction, and Apple/Google cuts. A mobile-optimized web app ships in days. Browser camera API is available if camera is added in v2. Solo devs should not be blocked by app store review for an unvalidated idea.

- **Added a visible Taste Profile screen.** The critique flagged that personalization feels like a black box — users don't trust what they can't see. A simple taste profile page showing inferred genres, favorite authors, and themes (editable) makes the AI feel transparent and trustworthy rather than random. This also gives users a reason to stay engaged even between book searches.

- **Added freemium monetization baked in from day one.** The critique called out no clear monetization path. v1 ships with a free tier (5 verdicts/month) and a paid tier ($4.99/month, unlimited verdicts + taste profile export). This is implemented from the start so the product is sustainable, not a free tool that burns out the builder.

## Improved Description

BookVerdict is a mobile web app that tells you — specifically you — whether a book is worth your time. Search by title, get a personalized verdict in under 30 seconds written as if a well-read friend who knows your taste is talking to you directly.

Onboarding takes 60 seconds: swipe through 15 popular books across genres (loved / meh / haven't read). The app builds your taste profile silently from those swipes — no genre dropdowns, no surveys. After onboarding, every verdict is generated by combining the book's metadata (Google Books API) with your taste profile fed into an LLM prompt that produces a single opinionated paragraph and a bold Read It / Skip It / Maybe Later call-to-action.

Users see a Taste Profile screen showing what the AI thinks they like (editable), a verdict history screen, and a simple settings page. The freemium model gates heavy usage behind a $4.99/month subscription from launch.

The web-first approach means no App Store friction. It runs in any mobile browser, can be added to the home screen as a PWA, and ships weeks faster than a native app.

## Why This Is Worth Building (Solo + AI)

A solo dev with AI coding tools can ship the core loop — onboarding quiz, title search, LLM verdict — in under 2 weeks because every piece (Google Books API, LLM call, swipe UI) has well-documented patterns and AI coding assistants can scaffold them fast. The cold start fix (swipe quiz) and the verdict format (opinionated paragraph) are the two decisions that separate this from a Goodreads wrapper, and both are design/prompt decisions, not engineering complexity. Freemium monetization baked in from day one means the builder has a path to sustainability without a second product redesign.

## Solo MVP Scope

- **What's in v1:**
  - Onboarding swipe quiz (15 curated books, loved/meh/haven't read)
  - Title search via Google Books API with cover previews
  - LLM-generated personalized verdict paragraph + Read It / Skip It / Maybe Later CTA
  - Taste Profile screen (inferred from swipes, user-editable)
  - Verdict history (last 20 verdicts)
  - Freemium gate: 5 free verdicts/month, $4.99/month unlimited
  - Auth via magic link email (no password friction)
  - PWA manifest for home screen install

- **What's out of v1:**
  - Camera / book cover OCR (v2)
  - Social features, sharing, following friends
  - Reading lists / want-to-read tracking
  - Push notifications
  - Integrations with Goodreads or StoryGraph
  - ISBN barcode scanning
  - Mood-based filtering ("I want something cozy right now")
  - Native iOS/Android app

- **Estimated build time with AI coding tools:** 12–14 days

## Open Questions

- Which LLM prompt structure produces verdicts that feel genuinely personal vs. generic? Needs 20–30 manual test cases before launch.
- Does Google Books API have sufficient coverage for niche/small press books? Need to test with edge cases before committing to it as the sole metadata source. Open Library as fallback?
- What 15 books go in the onboarding quiz? They must span genres, be recognizable enough to swipe confidently, and not skew toward one demographic. This is a product decision that needs a deliberate list, not a random sample.
- Is $4.99/month the right price? Might be too cheap to signal quality or too expensive for casual readers. Could test $2.99 vs $4.99 at launch.
- How do you handle a book with almost no Google Books metadata (self-published, obscure foreign press)? Need a graceful fallback UX — "We don't have enough info on this book yet" — rather than a bad AI hallucination.

## Design Handoff

List every screen/view the app needs so a designer or AI design tool can start immediately:

- **Screen 1: Onboarding — Swipe Quiz**
  Purpose: collect initial taste data before first verdict. Shows one book at a time (cover, title, author). Three swipe/tap options: Loved It / Meh / Haven't Read. Progress indicator (e.g., 7 of 15). Skip option hidden or unavailable. Fast, visual, game-like. Data: pre-seeded list of 15 curated books with covers from Google Books.

- **Screen 2: Home / Search**
  Purpose: primary entry point after onboarding. Prominent search bar at top, "Search by title or author". Below: 2–3 recent verdict cards as context (shows this is a returning user experience). Minimal chrome. Data: user's last 3 verdicts from history.

- **Screen 3: Search Results**
  Purpose: show matched books after typing. List of results with cover thumbnail, title, author, year. Tap any result to generate verdict. Data: Google Books API search results (title, author, cover URL, ISBN, description, genres).

- **Screen 4: Verdict**
  Purpose: the core product screen. Shows book cover (large), title, author. Below: one personalized paragraph in conversational second-person voice. Bold CTA button: Read It / Skip It / Maybe Later (color-coded: green / red / yellow). Thumbs up/down on the verdict to improve future recommendations. Option to save verdict. Data: book metadata + LLM-generated verdict text based on user taste profile.

- **Screen 5: Verdict Loading State**
  Purpose: bridge between search tap and verdict delivery. Animated, has personality — not just a spinner. Could show "Reading your taste profile..." then "Thinking about [Book Title]..." as sequential steps. Target: under 5 seconds actual wait. Data: none displayed, just progress animation.

- **Screen 6: Taste Profile**
  Purpose: show the user what the AI knows about their taste. Inferred genres (e.g., "You lean toward: Literary Fiction, Sci-Fi, Historical"). Inferred authors they seem to like. Editable: user can remove a genre tag or add one manually. Explanation: "Based on your swipes and verdict feedback." Trust-building screen. Data: aggregated user preference model derived from onboarding swipes + verdict thumbs up/down history.

- **Screen 7: Verdict History**
  Purpose: list of all past verdicts. Each card: cover, title, verdict CTA result (Read It / Skip It / Maybe Later), date. Tap to re-read full verdict. Data: stored verdicts per user, sorted by date descending.

- **Screen 8: Paywall / Upgrade**
  Purpose: shown when free user hits 5-verdict monthly limit. Clear value prop: "Unlimited verdicts, built around your taste." Price: $4.99/month. Single CTA: Upgrade. Minimal friction. Data: user's current verdict count, plan status.

- **Screen 9: Settings**
  Purpose: account management, subscription status, feedback. Email shown, magic link re-send option, subscription status and manage link (Stripe portal), "Give feedback on a verdict" link, sign out. Data: user auth state, plan status.

**Key interactions:**

- Onboarding swipe quiz is mandatory on first launch — cannot skip to search. Swipes are stored locally first, synced on first auth.
- After onboarding, user lands on Home / Search. Every subsequent launch also opens to Home / Search.
- Tapping a search result immediately triggers verdict generation — no intermediate "confirm" screen. Verdict loading screen bridges the wait.
- Thumbs up/down on a verdict silently updates the taste profile in the background — no confirmation required, just a subtle animation acknowledgment.
- From Verdict screen: "Save" adds the book to history. "Share" copies a plain-text excerpt (no app required for the recipient).
- Taste Profile is accessible via a persistent nav item or profile icon — not buried in Settings.
- Paywall intercepts the 6th verdict attempt in a month and shows the upgrade screen inline, not as a separate navigation.

### Critique

**Verdict: WORTH BUILDING — Score: 7/10**

The v1-to-v2 iteration is solid: cutting camera, nailing the verdict format, and adding swipe onboarding were the three decisions that could have killed this. They were all fixed. The remaining risks are real but not fatal.

Key brutal points:
- **5 free verdicts/month is a conversion killer.** A reader browsing on a Sunday burns through 5 verdicts in 20 minutes and hits the paywall before they're hooked. Raise to 10 or run a 7-day unlimited trial.
- **15 swipes is borderline insufficient signal.** If 10 books are outside a user's genre, you have 5 data points and a generic first verdict. Expand to 20–25 with a sparse-signal fallback.
- **Prompt engineering is the entire product and it's underestimated.** Getting verdicts that feel genuinely personal — not horoscope-level — takes 3–4 days of testing, not a single sprint.
- **"Maybe Later" as a CTA undermines the core promise.** A decisive well-read friend doesn't say "maybe later." Cut it. Read It or Skip It only.
- **LLM cost + $4.99/month unlimited is a math problem.** A power user doing 100 verdicts/month costs you real money. Cap "unlimited" at 150 or price at $7.99.
- **Taste profile data structure must be designed before writing any code.** It feeds onboarding, verdict generation, thumbs feedback, and the profile screen. A bad structure creates cascading bugs.
- **Stripe + magic link auth together is 2–3 days of plumbing**, not an afternoon. This is the most underestimated part of the build.
- Realistic solo build estimate: **16–20 days**, not 12–14. Prompt quality iteration takes longer than expected every time.
