Verdict: KILL IT
Score: 3/10
What's Actually Good:
- Cutting posture detection was the right correction. It removes the most obviously bogus claim from v1, and it does not regress by sneaking posture inference back into the product.
- The bedtime-log → overnight recording → morning receipt loop is easy to understand, and the loudest clip is an immediate emotional payoff.
- Honest habit-tracker framing is less reckless than medical framing, and manual context tags are cheap to implement.
Brutal Feedback:
- Your “actual differentiator” is not a differentiator. SnoreLab already lets users select and create factors and remedies, compare them against Snore Scores, and inspect their impact in a Trends view. Its current App Store description explicitly advertises lifestyle-impact tracking, including alcohol. The claim that users need a spreadsheet to get this insight is simply false.
- This is now a stripped-down SnoreLab clone whose main novelty is prettier receipt copy. You are proposing to fight an incumbent that says it has tracked more than 170 million nights, while your detector is “audio amplitude + basic frequency analysis.” That is not a wedge; it is an accuracy downgrade wearing better branding.
- The hardest part is hand-waved as if it were plumbing. Recording audio is easy. Reliably distinguishing the user's snores from a partner, pet, fan, traffic, speech, coughing, blankets, and white noise is the product. A crude threshold will generate confident garbage, and adding “approximate” to the UI does not make garbage useful.
- “Sleeping alone” does not solve source attribution. It merely records whether one known source of contamination might exist. The app can still score a fan or dog, and on shared-bed nights the score and loudest clip may belong to the partner. Those polluted results then poison every correlation and experiment.
- The correlation reveal is statistically unserious. Seven nights split across five binary factors produces tiny, overlapping groups with massive confounding. “Your three worst nights all had alcohol” is a coincidence-sized observation, not a meaningful correlation. Late meals, late bedtimes, alcohol, congestion, weekday effects, phone placement, and sleep duration can all move together.
- The experiment mode overpromises too. Three nights before/after cannot isolate a factor when recording conditions, sleep duration, congestion, room noise, and natural night-to-night variance change. “Score dropped 30% — this factor seems to matter” converts noisy data into fake certainty, even without medical language.
- Why does the user come back after day 1? The answer is still weak: perform five seconds of bedtime admin for a week, tolerate questionable scores, and maybe unlock an insight the market leader already provides. Curiosity gets the first recording; there is no credible reason to switch or stay.
- Manual logging is not frictionless just because it takes five seconds. Bedtime is exactly when users are least interested in data entry. A default-on reminder is not retention; it is a notification users can disable.
- The 0–100 score invents precision without defining the denominator, normalization, calibration, or handling of different phone models and distances. A score that changes because the phone moved six inches makes the experiment loop meaningless.
- The proposed “loudest 10-second clip” may optimize for a dropped object, cough, partner speech, or alarm rather than snoring. Selecting a useful representative clip requires classification and confidence handling, not merely finding peak amplitude.
- Overnight iOS recording is UNVERIFIED for this implementation, so the score cannot exceed 7/10 under the stated rules anyway. Apple provides recording audio-session categories and locked-screen behavior, so this is not necessarily impossible, but interruptions, route changes, app termination, battery, storage, and real-device reliability still demand a spike before product work.
- The privacy burden is understated. An app that records bedrooms all night captures intimate speech and potentially another person who did not install the app. Local storage, deletion, retention, permission copy, clip export, crash logs, backups, and accidental cloud sync all need deliberate handling.
- The 2.5–3 week estimate confuses “screens exist” with “product works.” A solo developer can assemble recording, toggles, charts, and playback in that window. They cannot establish detector reliability across rooms and devices, validate scoring consistency, design honest low-confidence states, and test overnight failure modes in the same window.
- There is no business case. Monetization is explicitly cut, distribution is unanswered, the incumbent already owns the use case, and the product requires repeated real-world testing. Even a technically successful MVP proves almost nothing except that a phone can record sound overnight.
Key Questions:
- What exact user job does this perform that SnoreLab's existing factors, remedies, Trends view, recordings, and lifestyle-impact tracking do not?
- Why would anyone trust a new amplitude/frequency heuristic over an incumbent detector trained and refined across a claimed 170 million nights?
- Why does the user come back after day 1, before the seven-night unlock and after discovering that the same workflow already exists elsewhere?
- What minimum sample size and effect threshold prevent the app from presenting noise as a “pattern” or successful experiment?
- How will the app detect and discard nights contaminated by a partner, pet, fan, television, coughing, phone movement, interruptions, or inconsistent phone placement?
- Can overnight recording survive screen lock, interruptions, route changes, low storage, calls, alarms, app termination, and battery pressure on the oldest supported real devices?
- Is all raw bedroom audio processed and stored locally, for how long, and how can a user verify permanent deletion?
- What acquisition channel can reach people immediately after a partner complains about snoring without paying more than a zero-revenue app can recover?
Suggestions:
- Do not build the seven-screen app. Run a one-week technical spike that records overnight on one platform, produces candidate clips, and measures false positives against manually labeled audio. Kill it if the detector cannot produce consistently useful clips.
- If you continue, stop pretending factor correlation is novel. Find a genuinely underserved wedge through user interviews or abandon the category. “A nicer receipt” is packaging, not a product moat.
- Reduce the validation prototype to three views: start/log, recording status, and morning clip review. Remove Patterns, Experiment Mode, Calendar, and 0–100 scoring until users demonstrate repeated use of accurate recordings.
- Replace causal-sounding summaries with raw, minimum-sample comparisons and explicit uncertainty. Never say a factor “seems to matter” after three nights.
- Pick one native platform. Cross-platform audio abstractions plus overnight lifecycle debugging are a trap for a solo vibe-coded MVP.
- Test willingness to return before building correlations: recruit 10 target users, have them log and record for seven nights, and measure completed nights, clip accuracy, and whether anyone changes behavior. Do not count reminder opens as retention.
- Treat privacy as core scope: on-device processing, explicit retention controls, automatic raw-audio deletion, and clear warnings about recording other people.
- The rational recommendation is still to kill this version. The incumbent already has the feature claimed as the breakthrough, leaving you with worse detection, weaker trust, no distribution, and no monetization.
Solo Dev Reality Check:
- Can one person ship this in 2-4 weeks with AI coding tools? MAYBE — one person can ship a fragile single-platform demo with recording, toggles, a heuristic score, and charts. One person cannot validate a trustworthy snore detector, meaningful correlations, overnight reliability, and privacy behavior in that window; a credible MVP is closer to 6–10 weeks plus repeated real-world data collection.
- Biggest solo complexity traps: reliable locked-screen overnight capture; interruption and route-change recovery; audio file size and battery use; snore-versus-noise classification; partner/source attribution; cross-device microphone variance; consistent score calibration; representative clip selection; tiny-sample statistics and confounding; low-confidence and failed-night UX; bedroom-audio privacy and deletion; bedtime logging compliance; real-device overnight test cycles; competing with an incumbent that already ships the alleged differentiator
