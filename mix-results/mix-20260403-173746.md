### Idea 1: VoiceScore

**Targets:** Attractiveness

**Copies:** UMax — proved people will pay for an AI attractiveness score when the feedback is axis-by-axis, specific, and actionable rather than a vague compliment

**Blocks used:** Microphone sensor - AI voice analysis - multi-axis score reveal

**Concept:** You record 30 seconds of yourself talking normally — to a friend, on a call, reading aloud — and the app scores your voice across four attractiveness axes: warmth, confidence, energy, and pace. It shows which axis is dragging your overall score and gives you one targeted drill to fix it. The loop is record, see the breakdown, do the drill, re-record and watch the number move.

**Reveal moment:** Your voice scores 5.8/10 overall — confidence is 4.1, dragged down by upward inflection on declarative sentences — here is the 60-second drill.

**The hook:** I recorded myself talking and found out I sound uncertain and low-energy on every call — and there was a number that proved it.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — microphone + Whisper transcription for pace/filler analysis + GPT-4o audio API for tone classification; the hardest part is calibrating the scoring axes so results feel accurate rather than arbitrary.

### Idea 2: FriendScore

**Targets:** Fear of Loss

**Copies:** Dex Personal CRM / Covve — combined $10M+ ARR, proved people pay to track relationship health when the alternative is quietly losing people they care about

**Blocks used:** Contact data input - decay algorithm - streak + reconnect mechanic

**Concept:** You add your 20 closest friends once, log how you actually connected (call, hangout, coffee, text exchange), and the app calculates a friendship health score per person that decays over time. Friendships you think are solid start showing a decay score in red when you have not actually talked in 30+ days. The loop is log a real connection, watch scores recover, get a weekly "who is drifting" alert with one-tap reconnect prompts.

**Reveal moment:** Three people you consider close friends have drifted below 40% health — your last real conversation with the top one was 52 days ago.

**The hook:** It showed me I had not actually talked to my best friend in six weeks — I thought we were close because we reacted to each other's stories.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — contacts import for the list, manual logging for connections (no iMessage API needed), decay math is straightforward; the hardest part is making logging feel light enough that it does not become a chore.

### Idea 3: DecayTracker

**Targets:** Fear of Loss

**Copies:** Duolingo — $500M+ ARR, proved that skill decay anxiety (the "owl guilt") drives daily returns more reliably than any reward mechanic when users have already invested time in a skill

**Blocks used:** Self-reported practice log - spaced repetition decay curve - skill health dashboard

**Concept:** You add any skill you are actively building — an instrument, a coding language, a fitness technique, a second language — and log when you practice and for how long. The app runs a retention decay curve behind each skill and surfaces a health percentage that drops visibly when you skip days. The reveal is not a streak counter but a number going down, which is psychologically harder to ignore. The loop is log practice, watch retention hold, miss a session, see the number fall, come back.

**Reveal moment:** Piano: 58% retained — you practiced 21 days ago, and 15 minutes today recovers it to 84%.

**The hook:** It gave me a number for skills I spent months building that was quietly shrinking every day I skipped — and I could not look away from it.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — pure self-report, no external APIs or sensors needed; the hardest part is getting the decay curve calibrated believably so users trust the percentage rather than dismiss it as made-up.
