### Idea 1: BrowLine

**Targets:** Attractiveness

**Copies:** UMax — proved people will pay for a blunt looks verdict and a clear "fix this first" action

**Blocks used:** Camera - Object recognition - Share

**Concept:** You take one front-facing selfie in bathroom light and the app maps brow symmetry, arch, and eye-framing, then overlays the exact hairs to remove or leave alone for a cleaner, more expensive-looking brow. The onboarding sells one fear: stop making your face look worse with uneven trims before work, dates, or photos. It is a better version of generic beauty try-on apps because the output is one usable grooming overlay, not a pile of fake styles.

**Reveal moment:** A split-screen marks "remove 6 hairs here, keep this arch" and shows the cleaned-up brow making your whole face look sharper.

**The hook:** It gives you the eyebrow cleanup map people usually pay a salon to eyeball.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — the hardest part is keeping face-landmark overlays stable across messy lighting and different skin tones without pretending the app knows universal beauty truth.

### Idea 2: LensLock

**Targets:** Health

**Copies:** Alarmy — proved forced action plus visible consequences creates sticky daily behavior people will pay for

**Blocks used:** NFC - Consequences - Streak

**Concept:** You stick a cheap NFC tag on your contact-lens case once, tap when you put your lenses in, and the app starts a wear-time clock that turns hostile near bedtime if you have not tapped the case to confirm removal. It shows your red-eye risk window, escalates lock-screen alerts, and protects a clean-eye streak so the loop becomes tap in, get warned, tap out, avoid the lazy mistake. It is better than generic reminder apps because it enforces one scary nightly moment instead of sending another passive notification you ignore.

**Reveal moment:** A red screen says, "You've worn these lenses 15h 42m. Sleep in them tonight and tomorrow shifts into high-irritation risk."

**The hook:** It turns the stupid bedtime shortcut that wrecks your eyes into a visible clock you have to actively clear.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — the hardest part is making the one-time NFC setup simple enough that users actually adopt it on night one.

### Idea 3: TrayDebt

**Targets:** Fear of Loss

**Copies:** Alarmy — proved people pay when the app turns a vague future penalty into an immediate, unavoidable consequence

**Blocks used:** NFC - Streak - Consequences

**Concept:** Adult aligner users tap their tray case when they remove and reinsert aligners, and the app keeps a brutally simple daily wear-debt meter instead of a vague "wear 22 hours" reminder. Fall behind and it shows how much progress slippage you are risking on the current tray, then nags until you recover the time, so the loop becomes wear, tap, protect progress, repeat. It is a better solo-dev wedge than a full orthodontic companion because it sells one fear: stop paying thousands to delay your own result.

**Reveal moment:** A blunt screen says, "You are 4h 55m short today, which is enough to push this tray into tomorrow."

**The hook:** It makes invisible Invisalign slippage feel like a late-fee meter on your own smile.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — the hardest part is reducing logging friction enough that users actually tap the case every time.
