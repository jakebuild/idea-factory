### Idea 1: Signal Portrait

**Blocks used:** WiFi (networks/signal) - Procedural generation - Collect

**Concept:** Every WiFi network you join generates a unique procedural abstract painting — seeded from the network name, signal strength, and time of day. Your home WiFi is one painting. The coffee shop is another. The airport is another. The app builds a silent visual diary of every place you've inhabited, rendered as generative art you never had to create.

**Why it works:** *Your data IS the content* — the user does nothing except live their life. The collection mechanic gives a reason to revisit: "I've been to 47 places this month." The paintings are deterministic (same WiFi = same base painting), so sharing is meaningful — your home has a fingerprint only you have.

**The hook:** "Every place I go has a painting — my home WiFi looks like a storm and the office looks like a sunset."

**Solo ship check:** Yes — WiFi SSID access is a standard iOS/Android API call; procedural art can be built with a seeded noise algorithm (p5.js or HTML5 canvas) in a weekend. No backend needed. Hardest part: making the paintings beautiful enough that people want to collect them, which means investing real time in the generative art parameters.

### Idea 2: Weekly Roast

**Blocks used:** Screen time (data) - LLM - Share

**Concept:** Every Monday morning, the app reads your Screen Time stats and Claude writes a brutal, personalized comedy roast of your phone habits from the past week. "You spent 4 hours on TikTok and 6 minutes on your meditation app. Bold strategy." The roast is formatted as a shareable card — one tap to post to X or Instagram. The worse your habits, the funnier the card.

**Why it works:** *Your data IS the content* + *shareable moments* — self-deprecating content performs. People post their Spotify Wrapped because "look how embarrassing I am" is socially rewarded. Screen Time data is the most honest measure of who you actually are vs. who you think you are. The roast turns guilt into entertainment. Alarmy proved consequences can be funny — this extends that to passive data.

**The hook:** "My phone told me I spent 3 hours doomscrolling Reddit and it roasted me harder than my therapist."

**Solo ship check:** Yes — Screen Time API is available on iOS via ScreenTime framework (requires Family Controls entitlement, which Apple approves for parental/self-monitoring apps). LLM call to Claude with the data takes an afternoon to prompt-engineer well. The shareable card is a Canvas render. Hardest part: the Screen Time entitlement approval from Apple, which requires a specific justification and can take 1-2 weeks.

### Idea 3: Sound Garden

**Blocks used:** Microphone - Watercolor (fluid simulation) - Streak - Evolve

**Concept:** Record 30 seconds of audio each day — your morning commute, your kitchen, your voice, ambient silence. The audio's characteristics (volume peaks, pitch distribution, rhythm) drive a watercolor painting of a single flower that blooms into your garden. Loud chaotic audio = bold reds and oranges. Quiet, low-pitch audio = soft blues and greens. After 30 days your garden is a visual sound diary of your month. Miss a day and the newest flower wilts but the garden survives.

**Why it works:** *Collection* + *shareable moments* + *your data IS the content* — the garden is uniquely yours because no two people's daily audio is the same. The streak mechanic is forgiving (wilted flower, not reset), which avoids the rage-quit dynamic. Watercolor output is inherently beautiful and social-ready. The 30-second daily recording is low enough friction that it can become a genuine ritual.

**The hook:** "I recorded 30 days of sounds and my garden looks exactly like my life — loud and colorful on weekends, soft and grey on Mondays."

**Solo ship check:** Yes — microphone access and audio analysis (volume, pitch via FFT) are well-documented on both platforms. Watercolor rendering can be approximated with layered translucent brush strokes on canvas (no fluid simulation needed — fake it with opacity and blur). Hardest part: making the audio-to-color/shape mapping feel meaningful rather than arbitrary, which requires tuning parameters against real recordings.
