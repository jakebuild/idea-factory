### Idea 1: FaceCard

**Targets:** Attractiveness / Fear of Making a Bad First Impression

**Copies:** Photofeeler — 3M+ users, $5M+ ARR, proved people pay repeatedly to crowdsource ratings on their profile photos before posting them

**Blocks used:** Camera / photo upload - AI face & composition analysis - score reveal with actionable improvement tips

**Concept:** You upload your LinkedIn or dating app profile photo and the app gives you an instant AI-scored First Impression Report — measuring how trustworthy, competent, and likeable you appear in that specific photo. Rather than a raw attractiveness score, it surfaces the exact composition and expression cues holding you back (head angle, smile, eye contact, background noise) and shows a predicted score lift if you fix each one. The loop is upload → get your score → follow one fix → re-upload to beat it.

**Reveal moment:** A bold card appears: "Your photo reads 6.1 / 10 for Trustworthiness — moving 6 inches closer to the camera would add ~1.4 points"

**The hook:** You send your score card to a friend and say "rate yours" — because now you're both self-conscious about every profile photo you've ever posted.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — OpenAI Vision API handles image analysis; scoring rubric is a prompt; hardest part is making the incremental score improve consistently when users re-upload with the suggested fix.

### Idea 2: WealthAge

**Targets:** Financial Anxiety / Fear of Falling Behind Financially

**Copies:** Credit Karma — 130M users, $1.8B acquisition by Intuit, proved people obsessively check a financial health number even when the news is bad, and return daily for free

**Blocks used:** Manual data input (income, savings, net worth) - AI wealth trajectory scoring - peer comparison reveal

**Concept:** You input 4 numbers — monthly income, monthly savings, total debt, and net worth — and the app calculates your Wealth Age: how old your financial habits make your money behave compared to Federal Reserve age-cohort benchmark data. A 28-year-old with the savings rate of a 44-year-old sees it immediately. The loop is update your numbers monthly, watch your Wealth Age trend down, and share your score milestone when you "de-age" by a year.

**Reveal moment:** A single bold number fills the screen: "Your money habits are 14 years behind your actual age — here is the one change that cuts that gap in half."

**The hook:** It's the only financial app that makes you feel genuinely behind or genuinely ahead using a single number — and both outcomes make you tell someone.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — no Plaid required in v1; Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances is public benchmark data; hardest part is calibrating age cohort brackets to feel accurate without being discouraging enough to churn users.

### Idea 3: BrainPace

**Targets:** Cognitive Anxiety / Fear of Early Mental Decline

**Copies:** Lumosity — 100M+ users, $50M+ ARR, proved people pay $80/year to track cognitive performance scores against a personal baseline over time

**Blocks used:** Simple timed interaction (reaction test, pattern recall) - score reveal with trend tracking - streak mechanic

**Concept:** Each morning you complete a 60-second two-part test: a reaction speed tap test and a 5-item working memory sequence. The app calculates a Cognitive Pace Score and maps it against an age-adjusted baseline — showing you whether your brain is tracking fast, average, or slow for your demographic. The key departure from Lumosity is the 60-second hard cap and a single trending number, not a suite of games. The loop is test daily, see the trend, notice your score dips on bad sleep nights, and obsess over recovering it.

**Reveal moment:** Score appears with a trend line and a callout: "Your reaction speed tested 11% slower than your 30-day average — your last 3 low scores all followed nights under 6 hours of sleep."

**The hook:** You share your score when you hit a personal best, and your friends download the app to see if they score better — because nobody believes someone else has faster reactions than they do.

**Solo ship check:** Yes — reaction tap test and pattern recall are pure SwiftUI with no external APIs; hardest part is calibrating age-adjusted norms from published cognitive science literature so the score feels grounded, not arbitrary.
