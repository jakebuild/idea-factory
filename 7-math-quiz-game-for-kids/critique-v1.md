Verdict: NEEDS MAJOR WORK
Score: 2/10

What's Actually Good:
- Kids genuinely do need math practice, so demand exists in some form
- The market is proven — parents and teachers actively seek these tools
- Low technical complexity means it's actually buildable by one person

Why It Might Fail:
- This is the single most saturated EdTech category on earth. Khan Academy Kids, Prodigy, Splash Learn, Mathway, DragonBox, Coolmath Games, and literally thousands of App Store clones already do this. "Math quiz game for kids" is not an idea — it's a genre.
- The description is five words. There is no differentiation, no target age, no unique mechanic, no monetization angle, no insight about WHY existing solutions are failing kids. You haven't described an idea, you've described a category.
- Parents don't buy "math quiz games." They buy solutions to specific pain points: my 7-year-old hates multiplication, my kid is behind in fractions, screen time that teachers will approve. Without a specific pain point, you have no positioning.
- Teachers have locked-down iPads and strict procurement processes. Consumer parents are already overwhelmed with free alternatives. Who exactly is paying for this, and why?
- Engagement is the graveyard of EdTech. Kids play it twice, get bored, uninstall. Without a retention loop (progress narrative, social mechanics, curriculum alignment), it's abandoned software.
- Zero moat. Even if you build it, anyone can clone it in a weekend. Prodigy spent years building curriculum alignment and school partnerships — that's the actual moat. A quiz loop has none.
- App Store discoverability for kids' education apps is brutal. The top results are entrenched with thousands of reviews. You will spend more on user acquisition than you'll ever make.

Brutal Questions To Answer First:
- What specific age range and math skill are you targeting, and why is every existing solution failing that child right now?
- What is the ONE thing this does that Prodigy, Khan Academy Kids, or Coolmath does not? Not "it's simpler" — something genuinely differentiated.
- How does a child discover this app, and what is your customer acquisition cost vs. lifetime value?
- Are you targeting parents (consumer), teachers (B2B), or schools (enterprise)? Each is a completely different business model and sales motion.
- What keeps a kid coming back on day 3, day 7, day 30? What is the retention mechanic?
- Have you talked to a single parent, teacher, or kid about this? What did they say?
- Why are you the person to build this? Do you have curriculum expertise, a kid in this age range, or a distribution channel?

Suggestions If You Proceed:
- Niche down brutally. "Math quiz game for kids" is not a product. "Multiplication table mastery for 7-year-olds with ADHD, using 60-second daily streaks" is a product.
- Find a distribution angle before writing a line of code. A partnership with one school district, a TikTok mom audience, or a homeschool Facebook group is worth more than any feature.
- Validate with a no-code prototype (Google Form quiz, a printable sheet, anything) before building. If you can't get 20 kids to use a paper version, you can't get 20k to download an app.
- If you want a technical edge, pick a specific mechanic nobody has nailed: spaced repetition timed to school curriculum calendars, parent dashboard with specific gap identification, voice-first for pre-readers. One sharp mechanic beats a polished generic app.
- Seriously consider whether this should be a feature inside an existing platform (a Notion template for homeschoolers, a Google Classroom add-on) rather than a standalone app.
