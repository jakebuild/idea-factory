Verdict: WORTH BUILDING
Score: 6/10

What's Actually Good:
- The v2 sharpening is real. "7–9 year olds with ADHD struggling with multiplication" is a named, searchable pain point — not a genre. Parents of ADHD kids actively spend money on things that promise to work when everything else failed.
- Distribution-first validation is textbook correct. A printable sprint sheet as a lead magnet costs nothing to make and directly tests whether parents care enough to act. This is the right sequence: validate demand before writing code.
- 60-second mechanic is genuinely differentiated. Nobody has built an app explicitly designed around a sub-2-minute attention window. Prodigy and Khan Academy are optimized for neurotypical session lengths. This framing is sharp and defensible in marketing copy.
- Parent-facing gap report (not a child-facing dashboard) is a smart UX insight. Parents of ADHD kids are used to being the case manager. Giving them a one-line daily signal ("Emma still misses 7×8") is respectful of their time and fits how they already think.
- B2C consumer motion is the right call. Avoiding school sales cycles is worth more than it sounds — schools take 6–18 months to buy anything, pay late, and churn with administrator turnover. Consumer parents who are already bought into solving this problem will pay $4.99 today.
- The open questions section is honest and self-aware. The founder already identified the Common Core/homeschool tension, the retention risk, and the "is SR just a shuffle?" question. That self-awareness is a good sign.

Why It Might Fail:
- The free tier kills the daily habit. 5 sprints/week means 2 missed days every week on the free tier. You are building a DAILY habit app and then structuring your monetization to break the daily streak unless they pay. A user who misses Wednesday and Thursday has a broken streak by Thursday. Broken streaks are the #1 reason habit apps churn. This is a self-inflicted wound.
- Common Core alignment is in direct conflict with the homeschool channel. Homeschoolers — especially the religious homeschool Facebook groups with 100k+ members — are disproportionately anti-Common Core. Mentioning "Common Core 3rd grade pacing" as a Pro feature to homeschool communities is not a selling point; it is a trust-destroyer and a ban trigger. The best feature and the best channel are in active conflict. This has not been resolved in v2.
- COPPA compliance is unpriced. The entire value proposition — tracking which multiplication facts each child misses and building a per-child spaced repetition model — requires collecting and storing performance data on children under 13. COPPA mandates verifiable parental consent flows, data deletion procedures, and regular policy review. This is not a checkbox; it requires legal review, engineering overhead, and ongoing compliance cost. A solo developer who hasn't priced this in is sitting on a liability.
- "Return the sheet" validation is weaker than it sounds. Downloading a PDF ≠ completing and submitting it. The funnel from "parent downloads printable" to "parent brings it to the child, completes it, and returns results" probably converts at 5–10%. To get your 200 signal, you may need 2,000+ downloads, which is a real distribution challenge before you've proven anything about the app.
- ADHD parents are exhausted, and this product adds a daily task to their plate. The parent-driven trigger model (parent gets notification → brings child to device → runs sprint) sounds simple. In practice, getting an ADHD child to cooperate with a timed test before school, consistently, every morning, is not a 60-second lift for a parent who is already managing a difficult morning routine. The product assumes parent willingness that real-world ADHD family mornings will not sustain.
- This is a naturally time-limited product masquerading as a subscription. A child either masters multiplication tables in 3–6 months or they don't. Once they do, the reason to keep paying disappears. Seasonal churn (September subscribe, June cancel) means annual LTV per subscriber is probably $20–40. At 500 subscribers that's $10–20k/year — not a business. You need 2,000+ paying subscribers to cover COPPA legal costs, app store fees, infrastructure, and a founder's time. That's a much harder target than "viable at 500."
- The mechanism for capturing child performance is unspecified. Does the parent manually enter how many problems the child got right? Does the child tap answers on-screen? If it's parent-reported (especially for the printable sheet phase), the data is unreliable — parents will round up to encourage their kids. Garbage inputs → garbage spaced repetition model → the core "moat" is built on bad data.
- The spaced repetition moat is probably false complexity. The founder's own open questions admit this risk. For 12×12 = 144 multiplication facts, a weighted re-shuffle of missed problems is 90% as effective as a full SR engine and can be built in an afternoon. The moat is weaker than claimed, and the real moat (accumulated child gap data) only matters if users stay long enough to generate meaningful data — which requires solving the churn problem first.
- Facebook group distribution is fragile and unscalable. Group admins are aggressive about product promotion. One post framed wrong gets you permanently banned from your highest-value community. You get one shot per group. This is mentioned in open questions but there is no plan for it — organic seeding requires months of authentic community participation before any product mention, which is a real time cost for a solo founder.

Brutal Questions To Answer First:
- Have you personally spent time in ADHD parenting Facebook groups — not lurking, but posting and reading responses? Which specific groups have you engaged with, and what did they say when you described this problem?
- What is your realistic theory for how a parent gets an ADHD 7-year-old to sit down for a timed multiplication test before school, every morning, without a meltdown? "Kids can focus for 60 seconds" is true in a clinical setting. "ADHD kids will cooperate with a timed performance test initiated by a parent in a stressful morning" is a very different claim.
- Have you spoken to a lawyer about COPPA? The spaced repetition model is explicitly built on tracking a named child's performance over time. What is your compliance cost estimate, and is it in your unit economics?
- What does "return the sheet" mean operationally? How does a parent submit results? If it requires a photo upload or manual data entry, your 200-completions validation gate is much harder than it appears.
- After a child masters multiplication tables — which is the stated goal — why do they keep paying? Have you modeled what happens to your subscriber count 6 months after a cohort's kids succeed?
- You plan to target homeschool communities AND offer Common Core alignment as a Pro feature. Do you know that these two things are in conflict? What is your curriculum positioning for homeschoolers specifically?
- What is your COPPA-compliant answer to the question "how do you store and protect my 8-year-old's performance data?" Can you answer this today?

Suggestions If You Proceed:
- Fix the free tier before launch. Give unlimited sprints for free with a 7-day or 14-day streak cap, or make the limit "3 sprints per day" rather than "5 per week." Anything that lets a daily habit form without hitting a paywall in the first week. Gate the parent gap dashboard (the most parent-valuable feature) behind Pro, not the core habit mechanic.
- Split the curriculum positioning by channel. For homeschool communities: drop Common Core language entirely, use "aligned to 3rd grade multiplication fluency benchmarks." For school-adjacent parents: "Common Core 3rd grade pacing." Same feature, different framing. Do not cross the streams.
- Solve the performance capture problem before the spaced repetition engine. Decide: is this parent-reported (fast to build, bad data) or child-input (better data, more build time)? A web app where the child taps answers is probably the right answer, but it means the printable sheet validation won't give you clean SR data. Be explicit about this tradeoff.
- Add a natural "graduation" to extend LTV. When a child masters 12×12, unlock division facts, then fractions fluency. Position it as "Math Sprint: Level 2." This extends the natural product lifetime from 3–6 months to 12–18 months and significantly improves LTV without rebuilding the core mechanic.
- Price research: before committing to $4.99/month, test a one-time $7.99 purchase offer in your community posts. "Education subscription fatigue" is real, and a single purchase with a free trial may convert better in a community that has already paid for and abandoned Prodigy and Khan Academy subscriptions.
- Get COPPA guidance before writing any code that touches child data. Even a 30-minute consultation with an attorney who knows EdTech will tell you what you're actually on the hook for. This is not optional.
- For the Facebook group channel, lead with the problem, not the product. Post: "We've been struggling to get our 8-year-old with ADHD to practice multiplication for more than 30 seconds. We made a 60-second sprint sheet — here's a free printable if anyone wants to try it." That is not a product pitch; it is a parent sharing a resource. That framing survives admin scrutiny.
