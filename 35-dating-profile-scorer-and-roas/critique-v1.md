Verdict: NEEDS MAJOR WORK
Score: 4/10
What's Actually Good:
- The hook is instantly understandable. "Why am I getting ghosted?" is emotionally charged and easy to market in one sentence.
- An MVP is technically simple enough for a solo developer: text input, photo upload, LLM analysis, payment gate, and a share card.
- The roast angle gives the product a sharper personality than generic "AI dating coach" apps.
Brutal Feedback:
- This is not a business yet. It is a prompt wrapped in a landing page. "AI scores your profile and rewrites your bio" is thin, trivial to copy, and already crowded by ChatGPT, Claude, Reddit, and a thousand low-effort dating helper tools.
- The core score is fake precision. A 1-10 number implies objective truth when the model is just inventing confidence around subjective dating preferences. Users will argue with it, not trust it.
- "Tells you exactly why you're getting ghosted" is pure overclaim. The app has never seen their matches, chats, or actual outcomes. It is guessing from a profile and pretending it knows causality.
- The roast positioning is a double-edged weapon. It may grab attention, but it also increases the chance the output feels mean, inaccurate, or emotionally harmful. That is how you turn curiosity into refunds, complaints, and screenshot-driven backlash.
- Photos are the real product risk. Once you accept face photos, you inherit privacy anxiety, moderation issues, possible NSFW uploads, sensitive attribute inference risk, storage/security expectations, and much higher user hesitation. That is a lot of baggage for a $3.99 gimmick.
- The monetization is weak. A one-time $3.99 unlock for photo tips is tiny revenue attached to a use case people may only do once. If the free rewrite is good enough, they leave. If the free rewrite is bad, they also leave.
- The viral loop is wishful thinking. Most people do not want to publicly post an AI-generated report card saying their dating profile sucks. Humiliation is not a reliable growth engine.
- TikTok distribution is not a strategy. It is a prayer. If the share card is not genuinely funny, surprising, and safe to post, there is no loop. If it is too mean, creators will avoid it. If it is too soft, nobody shares it.
- Retention is weak to nonexistent. Why does the user come back after day 1? They upload once, get roasted once, maybe buy once, and disappear. This is a disposable novelty, not a habit product.
- The product assumes users want brutal honesty, but many actually want validation plus actionable edits. If the roast is too soft, the gimmick dies. If it is too harsh, trust dies. That is a narrow tuning window.
- The bio rewrite being free likely destroys your paywall. You are giving away the clearest utility and charging for the fuzzier, lower-trust part.
- The scoring engine will be inconsistent across demographics, regions, orientations, and app contexts. Dating norms are not universal. Without careful segmentation, the advice becomes generic sludge.
- If you ever want credible photo optimization, "AI thinks this looks confident" is not enough. Users will expect before/after evidence, pattern libraries, or real profile performance data. You have none.
- The product has no moat, no proprietary data, and no believable path to becoming more accurate over time unless you collect outcomes. Collecting outcomes means more product complexity, more privacy burden, and more drop-off.
- Customer acquisition math likely sucks. If you need paid traffic, a $3.99 one-time purchase leaves no room for error. If you need organic, the concept must be irresistibly shareable, and that is unverified.
- The most likely real-world outcome is a short burst of curiosity, a mediocre conversion rate, a pile of edge-case moderation issues, and a dead app with decent screenshots.
Key Questions:
- What is the single insight this app can provide that ChatGPT with "roast my dating profile" cannot provide in 30 seconds for free?
- Why does the user come back after day 1?
- Are users actually willing to upload dating photos to a random new app, or does trust collapse before the first analysis?
- Is the roast tone a growth lever or just a novelty wrapper around generic advice?
- What proof will make the photo tips credible instead of feeling like AI fortune-cookie nonsense?
- Can this work without storing photos long term, and if not, are you prepared to handle the privacy expectations that follow?
- Are you optimizing for entertainment, actual profile improvement, or virality? Right now it tries to be all three and will likely be mediocre at each.
- If the share card is core to distribution, what specifically is share-worthy: the insult, the glow-up, or the before/after transformation?
Suggestions:
- Cut the product down to one sharp promise: rewrite bios and prompts for a specific dating app/persona, or roast profile text only. Do not start with photos unless you have a strong reason.
- Replace the fake universal score with a clearer structure like "first impression," "specificity," "signal of personality," and "matchability." That feels less arbitrary and more actionable.
- Charge for transformation, not vague tips. Example: free quick audit, paid full profile rewrite pack with multiple bio variants, prompt openers, and app-specific recommendations.
- Make it outcome-oriented. "Get 3 better bios for Hinge" is stronger than "AI gives you a 6.4/10 and dunks on you."
- If you keep photos, treat them as optional and temporary. Analyze then discard. Say that clearly, or users will bounce.
- Test shareability before building much. Mock five roast cards and see if real people would actually post them. Do not assume humiliation equals virality.
- Narrow the audience. For example: men on Hinge with poor prompt answers, or women rewriting bios after a breakup, or profiles for a specific market. Broad dating advice is generic mush.
- Consider a less disposable loop, such as weekly profile experiments, opener generation, match conversation feedback, or profile A/B testing. Otherwise this is a one-shot toy.
Solo Dev Reality Check:
- Can one person ship this in 2-4 weeks with AI coding tools? MAYBE — the app shell is easy, but photo handling, moderation, privacy messaging, payment flow, and getting outputs good enough that users trust them are where the fake simplicity collapses.
- Biggest solo complexity traps: photo upload/storage/deletion flows, NSFW or abusive image moderation, prompt tuning for "funny but not cruel," inconsistent scoring that angers users, weak payment conversion because the free part already solves the job, and a nonexistent retention loop.
