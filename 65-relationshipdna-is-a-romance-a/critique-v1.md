Verdict: NEEDS MAJOR WORK
Score: 4/10
What's Actually Good:
- The hook is brutally clear. “You dated the same person three times” is emotionally loaded, instantly legible, and naturally suited to screenshots and short-form video.
- Three relationship comparisons are more distinctive than another generic attachment-style quiz and give the reveal a satisfying beginning-middle-end structure.
- A stripped-down novelty MVP is technically small: structured intake, one constrained LLM analysis, a results page, and an exportable card. It does not require a marketplace or real-time network.
- The concept targets a real post-breakup urge: people desperately want a coherent story explaining why their relationships keep failing.
Brutal Feedback:
- This is a breakup horoscope wearing a lab coat. Six minutes of biased memories about three absent people cannot credibly identify attachment style, classify “emotional archetypes,” or explain a repeating causal pattern.
- The input is epistemically rotten. One hurt narrator summarizes complex relationships after the fact, selects which facts matter, and labels each ex. The model is measuring the user’s storytelling frame, not objective relationship dynamics.
- The promise demands an impossible combination: fast, specific, and psychologically responsible. Fast plus specific produces confident cold reading. Fast plus safe produces generic mush. Specific plus responsible requires a deeper, validated assessment that destroys the two-minute pitch.
- “Exactly how the pattern played out” is dishonest. The app has no messages, behavioral timeline, partner perspective, or validated instrument—only three compressed anecdotes and a model trained to produce plausible coherence.
- Attachment language borrows clinical authority without clinical validity. A disclaimer will not neutralize a polished card declaring “you are anxious and choose avoidants.” Users will remember the label, not the caveat.
- The growth mechanic rewards the worst output behavior. Absolute, provocative, blame-heavy statements spread better than cautious hypotheses, so virality directly conflicts with truthfulness and emotional safety.
- Why does the user come back after day 1? There is no convincing answer. The mystery is resolved in one sitting. Weekly prompts are homework disguised as retention, not a loop with fresh value.
- Inviting an ex is delusional as growth and reckless as product design. Most exes will ignore it; the rest may experience it as bait, accusation, harassment, or an attempt to reopen contact. It is especially dangerous around stalking, coercion, or abuse.
- Inviting a friend is also weaker than it sounds. Either the friend sees private allegations about exes, or they see a sanitized card too vague to validate anything. A reaction is not a recurring product loop.
- The share card may be screen-recordable, but “shareable” is an untested assumption. It advertises the user’s romantic failures and indirectly labels three third parties. Many users will save it privately, crop the brand, or refuse to post it.
- The free reveal gives away the only strong dopamine hit. The paid offer—more analysis, a “compatibility filter,” and weekly prompts—is less exciting and less trustworthy than the free result. The paywall comes after the product has already climaxed.
- The compatibility filter is pseudo-precision stacked on pseudo-precision. Recommending whom to date next from biased summaries of exes is fortune-telling, not compatibility science.
- The app collects unusually sensitive first- and third-party data: trauma, abuse, sexual history, mental-health claims, and potentially identifiable allegations. A solo vibe-coded product using model, analytics, logging, and payment vendors is a terrible trust proposition unless data minimization is exceptional.
- Safety is not a prompt footnote. Inputs will mention self-harm, violence, coercion, stalking, minors, revenge, grief, and abuse. Safe refusals, crisis routing, deletion, redaction, age gates, and third-party privacy all expand the project beyond a cute AI wrapper.
- Output quality—not UI—is the real build. Avoiding hallucinated evidence, contradictory labels, stereotypes, victim-blaming, and overconfident advice requires a hand-reviewed evaluation set and repeated testing. AI coding tools do not automate judgment.
- There is no moat. Anyone can paste three relationship summaries into ChatGPT and ask for patterns, then put the answer into Canva. Your defensibility is limited to superior intake, constrained analysis, tasteful output, safety, and distribution; none is proven.
- The likely business is high curiosity, low trust, one-session usage, weak subscription retention, and support/liability risk disproportionate to revenue. That is a bad shape for a solo developer.
Key Questions:
- Why does the user come back after day 1?
- Is this honestly reflective entertainment, or are you prepared to validate and operate a mental-health-adjacent assessment product?
- What behavioral rubric maps answers to claims, and how will you prove the model is doing more than personalized cold reading?
- What exact fields can capture enough evidence across three relationships while preserving the promised two-minute completion time?
- What makes a result meaningfully better than a purpose-built ChatGPT prompt and a Canva template?
- Why would anyone invite an ex, and how will you prevent unwanted contact, retaliation, harassment, or disclosure of third-party information?
- What do users pay for after receiving the strongest emotional payoff for free?
- How will users delete raw narratives, derived labels, generated reports, exports, logs, backups, and vendor-held copies?
- What are the kill thresholds for completion rate, branded share rate, referral conversion, paid conversion, harmful-output rate, and week-four retention?
Suggestions:
- Reframe the product as an entertainment-grade “relationship pattern reflection,” never an attachment diagnosis. Use tentative language and show the exact user-selected behaviors supporting every observation.
- Build only a one-shot web funnel: structured intake, two or three pattern hypotheses, evidence by relationship, a private editable card, payment for an actionable report, and immediate deletion. Cut accounts, subscriptions, ex invitations, and compatibility scoring.
- Replace free-form personality judgments with a compact behavioral rubric: pace, pursuit/withdrawal, conflict response, boundary respect, emotional availability, repair attempts, ending trigger, and the user’s own repeated behavior. Always allow “not sure.”
- Force symmetrical reflection. Ask what the user repeatedly did, tolerated, avoided, or escalated—not merely what was wrong with three exes. Otherwise this becomes an AI blame generator.
- Remove “invite an ex” entirely. If external perspective matters, let a trusted friend privately respond to user-authored pattern hypotheses without names, relationship details, or direct contact features.
- Test a paid one-time report rather than inventing a subscription. Weekly prompts do not justify recurring billing until retention exists in behavior, not a pitch deck.
- Create a hand-reviewed evaluation set before public launch covering vague and contradictory inputs, abuse, grief, revenge framing, self-blame, no detectable pattern, and users under 18. Track unsupported and harmful claims.
- Default to no real names, minimal retention, no model training, redacted telemetry, private results, explicit vendor disclosure, and one-click deletion. Do not store intimate narratives merely to make account screens feel populated.
- Run a concierge validation with 20–30 users before building the app. Manually review reports and measure completion, “felt specific but fair,” willingness to pay, branded sharing, and whether anyone wants a second session.
- Kill the concept if users enjoy the reveal but will not pay or share. That outcome means you built a party trick, not a business.
Solo Dev Reality Check:
- Can one person ship this in 2-4 weeks with AI coding tools? MAYBE — a solo developer can ship a narrow, private, one-shot web novelty with structured intake, constrained generation, a result card, export, deletion, and one-time payment. The full idea—credible attachment inference, ex invitations, compatibility guidance, subscriptions, robust moderation, and defensible privacy—cannot be shipped responsibly in that window.
- Biggest solo complexity traps: making a two-minute intake evidence-rich; developing and reviewing a safety evaluation set; constraining hallucinations and diagnosis-like certainty; handling abuse, stalking, self-harm, revenge, and minors; protecting intimate third-party data across model and analytics vendors; implementing complete deletion; rendering share cards reliably across devices; preventing invite abuse; discovering a paid offer after the best reveal is free; and confusing notification delivery with retention.
