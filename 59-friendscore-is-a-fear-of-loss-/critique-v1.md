Verdict: NEEDS MAJOR WORK
Score: 4/10
What's Actually Good:
- The hook is legible in one sentence: "your friendships are decaying and you can see which ones are slipping."
- A brutally stripped-down MVP is technically simple: friend list, interaction log, decay rules, weekly reminders.
- The underlying pain is real because adult friendships do drift and people do feel guilty about neglecting them.
Brutal Feedback:
- This is guilt-as-a-service. The product is basically a shame dashboard for people who already feel bad about being busy.
- The core mechanic is fake precision. "Friendship health score" sounds scientific, but it is just arbitrary math pretending to understand human intimacy.
- The app confuses measurable with meaningful. Days since contact is easy to count. It is not the same thing as relationship quality.
- The scoring will be wrong in obvious ways. Some close friends talk monthly and are fine. Some people text nonstop and are not emotionally close at all. Your flagship metric breaks on normal human behavior.
- Dex works because professional networking has clear ROI. This does not. There is no promotion, deal flow, or pipeline payoff here. You are asking people to do CRM for love and loyalty.
- The big reveal is a launch gimmick, not a product. "Three friends below 40%" gets a reaction once. After that, the user is staring at chores.
- Manual logging is the graveyard. If the user has to remember every hangout, call, or meaningful text exchange, your dataset rots within days.
- If you try to automate the logging, the whole differentiator becomes UNVERIFIED. Contacts are easy. Reliable message, call, and social metadata across platforms is not. That drags you into privacy friction, OS restrictions, brittle integrations, and edge-case hell.
- The retention loop is weak. Why does the user come back after day 1? Because a red number got worse? That is not habit-forming value. That is ambient guilt.
- Weekly drift alerts are not a product loop by themselves. If the action after the alert is still "do social maintenance manually," most people will swipe it away and feel slightly worse.
- The product may actively damage trust. If a user sees their best friend at 28% after a great weekend trip because they forgot to log two calls, the app looks stupid and insulting.
- The "likes are not real connection" line is good marketing copy and bad product logic. Users already know that. Knowing it does not make them want a separate app.
- Virality is mostly fantasy. People do not want to flex that they are neglecting their friends. Screen-recordable shame is not a stable acquisition channel.
- The likely user base is awkwardly split. Highly organized people may already have reminders or a notes system. Disorganized people are the least likely to maintain this long enough for the app to work.
- Worst case, you build an emotionally manipulative tracker that increases anxiety without creating better relationships. That is a very plausible failure mode.
Key Questions:
- What is the real value after the first reveal besides "you should reach out to someone"?
- Why does the user come back after day 1 when the novelty of the score is gone?
- Is the product manual-first or automation-first? If automation matters, what exact data source is actually available right now?
- Who is the first user niche that will tolerate relationship tracking instead of finding it creepy?
- How do you make the score feel useful rather than arbitrary, accusatory, or plainly wrong?
- What job does this do better than a recurring reminder plus a favorites contact list?
Suggestions:
- Cut the pseudo-scientific score and use plain-language drift signals like "you usually catch up every 3 weeks; it has been 7."
- Narrow the audience hard. Long-distance close friends, recent movers, or people maintaining 5-10 core relationships is plausible. "Everyone with friends" is lazy positioning.
- Make the MVP a weekly reconnection assistant, not a life dashboard: show 3 drifting friends, suggest one action, log it in one tap.
- Treat manual logging as the default reality and design around it instead of fantasizing about seamless passive tracking.
- Validate whether people will act on reminders for 4 straight weeks before building imports, scoring sophistication, or any AI summary fluff.
- If users only want lightweight nudges, accept the brutal truth that this may be a feature, not a company.
Solo Dev Reality Check:
- Can one person ship this in 2-4 weeks with AI coding tools? MAYBE — a manual reminder app is feasible, but the version that feels differentiated depends on trust, behavior design, and possibly UNVERIFIED data access, which is exactly where solo builders get buried.
- Biggest solo complexity traps: inventing a score users believe; preventing manual logging drop-off; avoiding privacy creep; deciding whether contacts import is worth the edge cases; building reminders that help instead of annoy; resisting the urge to chase call logs, SMS, social signals, and cross-platform syncing just to prop up a weak core loop.
