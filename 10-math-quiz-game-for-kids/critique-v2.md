Verdict: NEEDS MAJOR WORK
Score: 6/10

What's Actually Good:
- The pivot from "math quiz game" to "times table remediation for kids who just failed" is a genuine insight. Crisis moments convert. Parents who just got a bad report card are emotionally activated buyers — this is real.
- B2B2C via tutoring centers is smart. It's the right instinct to avoid the App Store wars and COPPA's most painful individual-child provisions. Tutoring centers already have trust with exactly the right parents.
- The parent dashboard as the retention mechanism is legitimately clever. Parents nagging their kids is more durable than any streak mechanic. Framing the parent as the retention agent is the most sophisticated thinking in this doc.
- Scope discipline in v1: multiplication tables only, no open world, no avatars. Rare and correct.
- The COPPA-safe monetization design (business as customer, not the child) shows someone has thought about the legal trap most edtech founders step into. Good.

Brutal Feedback:
- "Achievable with a weekend's integration work" is the most dangerous line in this document. Generating AI word problems for 7-year-olds isn't a weekend Claude API call — it's a content moderation nightmare. A prompt that produces "A Creeper drops 7 bombs 8 times" is already borderline. What happens when the model generates a problem about a child's interest in "knives" or "shooting games" or produces something subtly inappropriate at 2am when no one is watching? You need human review pipelines, keyword filters, age-appropriate output classifiers, and an incident response process. The moment one inappropriate problem reaches a 7-year-old through a tutoring center, you lose every tutoring center customer you have. This is not a weekend problem. It's a product safety problem that will eat your first 3 months.
- The free individual parent tier is a self-inflicted wound. You're asking tutoring center owners to pay $30–50/month while simultaneously offering the same product free to parents. The moment a parent tells their tutoring center "why are you charging us for this, I just use the free version at home?" — your B2B pitch collapses. Either kill the free tier entirely and go B2B-only, or make the individual tier so limited (3 problems/day) it's genuinely not competitive with the paid center experience.
- $30–50/month flat fee will destroy your unit economics at scale and leave money on the table at small scale. A tutoring center with 8 students is paying $6/student/month. Fine. A learning center with 80 students is paying $0.50/student/month. You've created an inverse incentive: your best customers (big centers) pay almost nothing per student, your worst customers (tiny centers) pay relatively more. Per-seat pricing ($2–3/student/month, minimum 10 seats) aligns incentives. You asked this question yourself in the open questions — the answer is obviously per-seat.
- Tutoring centers are a brutal sales channel for a solo founder. The owners are typically former teachers or parent entrepreneurs running 1–2 location businesses with no IT budget, no procurement process, and profound tech skepticism. Getting 10 centers signed will require in-person demos, one-on-one phone calls, and months of relationship building — not cold email. Your "first 100 users from 5–10 tutoring centers" assumption treats a 3–6 month sales grind as a gimme. It isn't.
- The AI word problem personalization is a feature, not a moat. This will be table stakes within 18 months. Duolingo already personalizes. Khan Academy has 50 engineers. The moment they add a "what does your child love?" onboarding question, your core differentiator disappears. What's the actual defensible position? The tutoring center relationships? The curriculum alignment? You need to answer this before building.
- Narrowing to multiplication tables only caps your revenue ceiling dramatically. The TAM for "tutoring centers who will pay for a multiplication-only app" is a fraction of the edtech market you're implicitly invoking. This might be a $300K–$800K ARR business at saturation in one metro, not a venture-scale play. That's fine if this is a lifestyle business, but the doc doesn't acknowledge this ceiling anywhere.
- Streaks are table stakes. Duolingo burned that mechanic into every parent's brain years ago. Mentioning it as a retention feature signals the product thinking here is still surface-level on the engagement side.
- The name "Times Table Rescue" communicates stigma directly to the child. You're labeling the kid as someone who needs rescuing. Parents may buy it, but the 8-year-old who has to open "Times Table Rescue" every day is being told every session they're behind. Consider whether the branding serves the actual end user.

Key Questions:
- What happens when the AI generates something inappropriate? Walk through the full incident response. Who reviews flagged content? How fast? What do you tell the tutoring center?
- Have you actually talked to 5 tutoring center owners? Not about the idea — about their current homework tools, their willingness to add new software, and what "sold" looks like for them?
- Why would a tutoring center use this instead of assigning pages from a $12 workbook? The workbook requires zero setup, has no privacy risk, needs no Wi-Fi, and never generates an inappropriate problem. What's the concrete answer to this objection?
- What does the curriculum alignment story look like? If a tutoring center uses Kumon or a specific Common Core-aligned curriculum, can you map your problem sets to their existing progression? This is what B2B edtech buyers actually ask about.
- Is the parent dashboard showing data the tutoring center owner doesn't also want? There's a potential conflict: if the center sees a student is practicing daily at home and acing it, they might lose a student. Have you thought through this incentive misalignment?

Suggestions:
- Kill the free individual parent tier in v1. Go pure B2B. Validate that tutoring centers will pay before you complicate the model with a consumer freemium layer. Add the consumer tier only after you have 10 paying centers.
- Switch to per-seat pricing immediately. $2.50/student/month, 10-seat minimum = $25/month floor. 50-student center = $125/month. This is more honest and scales with your customer's growth.
- Before writing a line of code, spend 2 weeks doing 20 in-person conversations with tutoring center owners. Bring a paper prototype. Your sales motion assumptions are completely untested.
- Solve the AI content safety problem in week 1, not as an afterthought. Build a human review queue for all generated problems until you have enough examples to train a classifier. Do not ship AI-generated content to kids without a human in the loop.
- Rename the product. The child-facing brand should feel empowering, not remedial. Call it something like "Times Champ" or "Math Squad" — the parent and tutoring center can know it's a remediation tool without broadcasting it in the app name the kid sees.
- Add one specific curriculum alignment claim to your pitch (e.g., "aligned to Common Core Grade 3 Operations & Algebraic Thinking standard 3.OA.C.7"). This single line will meaningfully increase conversion with tutoring centers who need to justify software to parents.
