Verdict: KILL IT
Score: 1/10

What's Actually Good:
- The target audience (kids) is well-defined and has real purchasing power via parents
- Math education is a proven market — Prodigy, Khan Academy Kids, Mathway all make real money
- Simple enough scope that a single dev could ship a v1 in a weekend

Brutal Feedback:
- This is not an idea. This is a category. "Math quiz game for kids" is the same level of specificity as "social network" or "food app." There is zero original thought here.
- The App Store and Google Play have THOUSANDS of math quiz games for kids. Prodigy has 50 million users. DragonBox raised $12M. You are entering a war zone with a spoon.
- No differentiation whatsoever. What makes this one different? Faster? Funnier? More adaptive? Tied to curriculum? You haven't said. That means you haven't thought about it.
- The entire "idea" is 6 words. There is no hook, no unique mechanic, no insight about why existing solutions fail kids. This is a shower thought, not a product idea.
- Parents are the actual customer, not kids. Kids don't buy apps. You have said nothing about how you reach or convince parents, who are extremely skeptical of screen time and educational claims.
- Without adaptive difficulty, kids either get bored (too easy) or frustrated (too hard) within minutes. Building adaptive learning properly is genuinely hard engineering. Have you accounted for that?
- Engagement cliff is brutal in kids' edtech. Without a reward loop, narrative, or social mechanic, kids abandon quiz apps in days. You have described nothing to address this.
- Teachers won't recommend it without curriculum alignment (Common Core, etc.). Parents won't trust it without some credibility signal. You have neither.
- Monetization is a minefield: kids' apps face COPPA compliance, no behavioral ads, no in-app purchases targeting minors. Your revenue model is non-existent in this idea.

Key Questions:
- What age range? A quiz for a 6-year-old is completely different from one for a 12-year-old.
- What is the one thing this does that Prodigy, Khan Academy Kids, or Splash Learn does NOT do?
- How do you acquire users? App Store SEO in this category is brutal.
- What is the monetization model given COPPA restrictions?
- Why would a parent choose this over the free options that already exist?
- What is the retention mechanic beyond the quiz itself?

Suggestions:
- Pick a brutally specific niche: "multiplication tables drill for kids who failed their times test" or "mental math speed trainer for math competition kids ages 8-12"
- Find a real parent pain point first — go talk to 10 parents about what actually frustrates them about their kid's math practice
- If you want to build this, add ONE unique mechanic: voice input, physical timer, multiplayer with siblings, AI-generated word problems from the child's interests
- Consider the B2B2C angle: sell to after-school programs or tutoring centers rather than trying to win the consumer app war
- At minimum, write 3 more sentences about what makes this different before investing any time in it
